The general election campaign is happening. The political parties involved all have policies and things, but let’s pretend for a moment that their so-called “battle buses” are important. Let’s investigate them.

Liberal Democrats

The first thing to make clear is this: none of the top three parties are using an actual bus; the phrase “battle bus” is only in use because of alliteration.

The Lib Dem battle bus in a building site in Watford, looking ready to take on the world… pic.twitter.com/vSyuaoo9Gv

— Tom Knowles (@tkbeynon) March 31, 2015

The Lib Dems’ bus is really a coach. OK, Wikipedia says a coach “is a type of bus”, and is “often simply called a bus”, but I maintain that “coach” is a much better word for vehicles like the Van Hool TX18 Altano. And if alliteration is so important, how about the phrase “campaign coach”?

Nowhere better to start the campaign than with the outstanding @laylamoran in Oxford West & Abingdon. Winning Here. pic.twitter.com/8fQBDKaRSN

— Nick Clegg (@nick_clegg) March 29, 2015

The Lib Dems’ coach belongs to Westway Coach Services, and photographs on Flickr show that most of the time, when not sheathed in yellow vinyl, it’s a rather bland colour.

The Telegraph has a poor article entitled “Nick Clegg’s battle bus has trouble turning left at campaign launch”. Many parts of the article are poor, such as the caption of this photograph:

First of all, there ought to be an apostrophe after “Lib Dems”. Secondly, the whole premise of the caption is wrong – the picture shows not a bus struggling to get down a road, but Nick Clegg waving as he stands in the doorway of a coach.

Interestingly, the Lib Dems also hired a Van Hool from Westway in the previous general election.
