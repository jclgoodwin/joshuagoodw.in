- Years ago, the consistency of Frank Cooper's Oxford marmalade changed and glucose-fructose syrup appeared in the list of ingredients. I was going to share the uplifting news that they've gone back to using normal sugar now, but [the website](https://www.frankcoopers.co.uk/our-products/original-oxford-marmalade/) lists glucose-fructose syrup and I don't think it's out of date, I think it means they've regressed to using the cheaper cornstarch-derived gloop again. The sainted Wayback Machine confirms that sugar was listed as recently as [April 2025](http://web.archive.org/web/20250420041529/https://www.frankcoopers.co.uk/our-products/original-oxford-marmalade/) and as long ago as [December 2023](http://web.archive.org/web/20231210080625/https://www.frankcoopers.co.uk/our-products/original-oxford-marmalade/), so I must savour the last good jar in my cupboard.

  (I know that the original marmalade [was originally made by Frank's wife Sarah-Jane](https://www.reddit.com/r/menwritingwomen/comments/pbxifw/so_frank_coopers_marmalade_isnt_actually_frank/), but it was named after Frank's shop where it was sold.)

- When I was a small child, we were shown around a local church by the churchwarden who showed us his [staff/stave/wand](https://allsaintswhitby.org/historical-notes-queries-a-word-about-wands/), a sort of ornate stick. Apparently, as well as having a ceremonial use, it could be used to break up fights or to prod sleeping churchgoers. Later, in a classroom I noticed a long wooden pole with a hook on the end, and I deduced that the teacher must be a part-time churchwarden, but no it was just a tool for opening high windows. What a funny story.
